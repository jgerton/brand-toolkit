# Donald Miller's StoryBrand Framework

Source: "Building a StoryBrand" by Donald Miller

## Core Principle

The customer is the hero, not your brand. Your brand is the guide. Every great story follows the hero's journey, and your marketing should too. If you confuse, you lose.

---

## The 7-Element BrandScript

### 1. A Character (Your Customer)

**Question:** Who is your customer, and what do they want?

The hero of your brand story is your customer, not you. Start by identifying what they want in relation to your brand. State it clearly and simply.

**Key:** One desire. Not five. The simpler and more specific, the better.

**Exercise questions:**
- What does your customer want as it relates to your product/service?
- What is their ultimate end goal?
- Can you state it in one sentence?

### 2. Has a Problem

**Question:** What problems are they facing?

Every story needs conflict. Define the problem on three levels:

- **Villain:** The root cause. Not your competitor, but the force your customer is fighting. (Complexity, wasted time, fear, inequity, etc.)
- **External Problem:** The tangible, surface-level issue. What they'd Google. What they'd describe to a friend.
- **Internal Problem:** The feeling the external problem creates. Usually: frustration, overwhelm, confusion, self-doubt, embarrassment.
- **Philosophical Problem:** Why it's just WRONG that they have this problem. "No one should have to..." or "It's wrong that..."

**Key insight:** Companies sell solutions to external problems, but customers buy solutions to internal problems. The internal problem is the emotional hook.

### 3. And Meets a Guide (Your Brand)

**Question:** How do you show up as the trusted guide?

A guide demonstrates two things:

- **Empathy:** "I understand your problem. I've been there."
- **Authority:** "I have the expertise/track record to help you." (Testimonials, credentials, logos, stats, experience)

**Key:** Lead with empathy, back with authority. Never lead with authority alone (that's a hero, not a guide).

### 4. Who Gives Them a Plan

**Question:** What's the simple plan to engage with you?

Reduce complexity. Give the customer a clear 3-step process:

1. Step one (usually: connect/schedule/sign up)
2. Step two (usually: the work/implementation)
3. Step three (usually: the result/transformation)

**Key:** Your actual process may have 15 steps. The plan you communicate has 3. Simplicity drives action.

**Two types of plans:**
- **Process plan:** Steps to buy or engage (reduces confusion)
- **Agreement plan:** Promises you make to remove fear (guarantees, commitments)

### 5. And Calls Them to Action

**Question:** What's the ONE thing you want them to do?

Two types of CTAs:

- **Direct CTA:** The ask. Buy now. Schedule a call. Start free trial. Make it prominent, repeat it.
- **Transitional CTA:** For those not ready. Download guide. Watch demo. Subscribe. ("Date me before you marry me.")

**Key:** Don't be timid. Ask clearly and repeatedly. The guide must challenge the hero to act.

### 6. That Ends in Success

**Question:** What does life look like after they choose you?

Paint a vivid picture of the customer's life after the transformation:
- What do they have that they didn't before?
- How do they feel?
- What's their new status?
- What problem is gone?

**Character transformation:** The hero goes from _________ to _________.

**Key:** Be specific. "More time" is weak. "Home by 5pm every day instead of working weekends" is strong.

### 7. And Helps Them Avoid Failure

**Question:** What happens if they DON'T choose you?

Stakes make stories compelling. What does the customer lose by not acting?

- What pain continues or worsens?
- What opportunity evaporates?
- What does their life look like 12 months from now without a solution?

**Key:** Don't overdo it (fear-mongering), but don't skip it. Without stakes, there's no urgency.

## The BrandScript Exercise

### Inputs Needed
- Deep understanding of your customer (from positioning work)
- Knowledge of the problem landscape (external, internal, philosophical)
- Your brand's guide credentials (empathy + authority)

### Worksheet

| Element | Your Brand |
|---------|-----------|
| **Character** | [Customer] wants [desire] |
| **Problem - Villain** | [Root cause they're fighting] |
| **Problem - External** | [Tangible problem] |
| **Problem - Internal** | [How it makes them feel] |
| **Problem - Philosophical** | [Why it's wrong] |
| **Guide - Empathy** | [How you understand] |
| **Guide - Authority** | [Why you're qualified] |
| **Plan** | 1. [Step] 2. [Step] 3. [Step] |
| **CTA - Direct** | [Primary action] |
| **CTA - Transitional** | [Lower-commitment action] |
| **Success** | [Life after transformation] |
| **Failure** | [What they lose by not acting] |

## Business-Type Adaptations

### SaaS
- **Villain:** Usually complexity, wasted time, broken workflows, or data silos
- **External:** "I can't get X done efficiently"
- **Internal:** Frustration, feeling behind, overwhelmed by tools
- **Plan:** Free trial > Onboard > See results

### Local Service
- **Villain:** Unreliable providers, DIY gone wrong, neglect
- **External:** "My [thing] is broken/ugly/dangerous"
- **Internal:** Embarrassment, stress, feeling like a bad homeowner/parent
- **Plan:** Get quote > We handle everything > Enjoy the result

### Content Creator / Community
- **Villain:** Information overload, gatekeeping, isolation
- **External:** "I don't know how to do X"
- **Internal:** Impostor syndrome, feeling lost, behind peers
- **Plan:** Join > Follow the path > Achieve [specific outcome]

### Ecommerce
- **Villain:** Compromise, settling, decision fatigue
- **External:** "I can't find [product] that meets my standards"
- **Internal:** Guilt, dissatisfaction, buyer's remorse
- **Plan:** Browse > Choose > Love it (or we fix it)

## Anti-Slop Checks for Messaging

1. **Is the customer the hero?** If any sentence starts with "We are..." or "Our company...", rewrite from the customer's perspective.
2. **Is the villain specific?** "Challenges" is not a villain. "Spending 6 hours a week on manual data entry" is a villain.
3. **Is the internal problem named?** If you only address the external problem, you're missing the emotional hook.
4. **Is the plan 3 steps or fewer?** Complexity kills conversion.
5. **Is the success picture specific?** "Better outcomes" fails. "Reclaim 10 hours per week" passes.
6. **Are there real stakes?** If nothing bad happens by not choosing you, there's no urgency.
