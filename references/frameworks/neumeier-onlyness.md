# Marty Neumeier's Onlyness Framework

Sources: "Zag" and "The Brand Gap" by Marty Neumeier

## Core Principle

A brand is a customer's gut feeling about a product, service, or company. It's not your logo or advertising. You influence that gut feeling by communicating what makes you different.

"If you can't say why your brand is both different and compelling in a few words, don't fix your positioning statement... fix your company."

---

## The Onlyness Statement

The litmus test for differentiation. If you can't keep it brief and use the word "only," you don't have a zag.

### Template

```
Our [offering] is the only [category] that [benefit].
```

### Extended Template (6 questions)

- **WHAT** is your category?
- **HOW** are you different?
- **WHO** are your customers?
- **WHERE** are they located?
- **WHEN** do they need you?
- **WHY** are you important?

### Example: Harley-Davidson

"The only motorcycle manufacturer that makes big, loud motorcycles for macho men (and macho wannabes) mostly in the US who want to join a gang of cowboys at a time of decreased personal freedom."

---

## The Trueline

An internal statement (not a tagline) that captures the one true thing about your brand, based on the onlyness statement. Competitors can't claim it. Customers find it valuable and credible.

**Trueline vs. Tagline examples:**

| Brand | Trueline (internal) | Tagline (external) |
|-------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Southwest Airlines | Lets you fly for the same cost or less than driving | You are now free to move about the country |
| Nike | Helps you find your inner athlete | Just do it |
| Disneyland | The world's favorite amusement park | The happiest place on Earth |

## The Zag 17-Point Blueprint (Key Points)

### Focus
1. **Who are you?** Core identity beyond products
2. **What do you do?** In plain language
3. **What's your vision?** Concrete picture of the future you're creating (not abstract)
4. **What wave are you riding?** What trend makes you more relevant now
5. **Who shares the brandscape?** Map the competitive territory
6. **What makes you the "only"?** The onlyness statement

### Difference
7. **What should you add?** What's missing from your offering that would strengthen your position
8. **What should you sacrifice?** What should you stop doing that dilutes your focus
9. **Who loves you?** Your brand's "unique buying tribe" (UBT), not USP

### Design
10. **How do you explain yourself?** In one sentence anyone can understand
11. **How do you spread the word?** Through actions, not just ads
12. **How do people engage with you?** Touch points and experiences

### Action
13. **What do people experience?** Map every brand touchpoint
14. **How do you earn their loyalty?** Beyond transactions
15. **How do you extend success?** Brand extensions without dilution
16. **How do you protect the brand?** Against dilution and drift
17. **Is your brand alive?** Does the whole company live it, not just marketing?

## Brand Tests

### The Swap Test
Take your brand icon, messaging, or visual. Swap in a competitor's name. If it works just as well or better, your branding needs work.

### The Hand Test
Cover your brand name on any marketing material. Can you tell whose it is from the remaining content alone? If not, your brand lacks distinctiveness.

### The Concept Test
Show brand elements to 10+ real audience members (not company insiders). Ask about understanding, not liking. Questions:
- Which promise is most valuable to you?
- What company would you expect to make this promise?
- If [company X] made this promise, would it make sense?

### The Field Test
Put the brand element in its real environment (product on shelf, ad in feed, website among competitors). Does it stand out?

## Key Concepts for Anti-Slop

### The Brand Gap
The rift between strategy (logic) and creativity (magic). Bridging this gap means your brand is both strategically sound AND creatively distinctive.

### Five Types of Clutter to Cut Through
1. **Product clutter:** Too many products/services
2. **Feature clutter:** Too many features per product
3. **Advertising clutter:** Too many media messages
4. **Message clutter:** Too many elements per message
5. **Media clutter:** Too many competing channels

### Charismatic Brand Definition
"Any product, service, or company for which people believe there's no substitute." These brands:
- Claim dominant category positions (50%+ market share)
- Command highest price premiums (up to 40% over generics)
- Are least likely to fall victim to commoditization

### Tribal Identity > Features
"In a marketplace of me-too offerings, people don't seek features and benefits so much as tribal identity. 'If I buy this product, what will that make me?'"

Focus on Unique Buying Tribe (UBT), not Unique Selling Proposition (USP).

## Business-Type Adaptations

- **SaaS:** Onlyness often found in unique workflow, integration pattern, or pricing model, not just features
- **Local service:** Geographic + specialization focus. "The only [service] in [area] that [specific approach]"
- **Content creator:** Personal brand angle. The "only" is often you + your specific perspective + your audience's identity
- **Ecommerce:** Product sourcing, curation angle, or customer experience differentiator
- **Community:** Tribe identity IS the product. Onlyness is about who belongs and why
