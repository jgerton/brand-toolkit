# Chris Do's Stylescapes Methodology

Source: Chris Do / The Futur

## Core Principle

A stylescape is a visual exploration of a brand's potential look and feel, presented as a curated mood board that goes beyond inspiration to convey a specific strategic direction. Unlike random Pinterest boards, stylescapes are intentional compositions that connect visual choices to brand strategy.

The key innovation: present 3 contrasting directions to give the client a real choice, not a false choice between "the one we want" and two throwaway options.

---

## The Process

### Step 1: Strategic Foundation

Before any visual work, you need:
- Positioning (Dunford's framework or equivalent)
- Target audience definition
- Brand personality / archetype (Aaker / Jung)
- Voice dimensions (NN/g framework)
- Competitive landscape understanding

**Visual identity without strategic foundation produces decoration, not branding.**

### Step 2: Create 3 Contrasting Directions

Each stylescape should feel like a different brand, not a slight variation. They represent genuinely different visual strategies.

**Direction types (choose 3 that contrast):**

| Direction | Visual Character | When to Consider |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Bold / Disruptive** | Strong colors, large type, breaking grids, unexpected imagery | Outlaw/Hero archetype, crowded market needing differentiation |
| **Minimal / Refined** | White space, restrained palette, elegant typography, photography-forward | Sophistication, premium positioning, Sage/Ruler archetype |
| **Warm / Approachable** | Soft colors, rounded elements, illustration, hand-drawn touches | Caregiver/Regular archetype, community-focused brands |
| **Technical / Precise** | Monospace fonts, grid-heavy, data visualization aesthetic, dark mode | Competence archetype, developer/technical audience |
| **Organic / Natural** | Earth tones, texture, natural photography, serif fonts | Explorer/Innocent archetype, sustainability-focused brands |
| **Playful / Energetic** | Bright colors, dynamic compositions, bold patterns, motion-feeling | Jester/Creator archetype, younger audience |

### Step 3: Compose Each Stylescape

Each stylescape includes:

1. **Color palette** (primary, secondary, accent, neutrals with hex codes)
2. **Typography pairing** (heading + body font with rationale)
3. **Photography/imagery style** (types of images, treatments, subjects)
4. **Texture and patterns** (if applicable)
5. **UI element hints** (button styles, card treatments, icon style)
6. **Sample copy** in the brand voice (not lorem ipsum)
7. **Logo direction** (type style, mark concept, not a final logo)

### Step 4: Present with Rationale

Each stylescape gets a strategic narrative:
- "This direction says [X] about your brand"
- "This would appeal most to [audience segment] because..."
- "This differentiates you from [competitors] by..."
- "The risk of this direction is..."

Never present visuals without strategy. The client should choose based on strategic fit, not personal taste.

### Step 5: Refine the Chosen Direction

Once a direction is chosen:
- Develop the full color system (not just 3-4 colors)
- Finalize typography with full hierarchy
- Create detailed imagery guidelines
- Produce design token documentation

## Color Psychology Reference

| Color | Associations | Best For | Avoid When |
|-------|-------------|----------|------------|
| **Blue** | Trust, stability, professionalism | Finance, tech, healthcare | You want to stand out from enterprise SaaS |
| **Red** | Energy, urgency, passion | Food, entertainment, retail | You want to convey calm or safety |
| **Green** | Growth, health, nature | Wellness, finance, sustainability | You want to feel cutting-edge or luxurious |
| **Yellow** | Optimism, warmth, attention | Children, food, creative | You want to feel sophisticated or serious |
| **Purple** | Luxury, creativity, wisdom | Beauty, spirituality, premium | You want to feel approachable or budget |
| **Orange** | Friendly, energetic, affordable | SaaS, food, youth | You want to feel premium or conservative |
| **Black** | Luxury, sophistication, power | Fashion, automotive, tech | You want to feel approachable or playful |
| **White** | Clean, minimal, modern | Tech, health, premium | You need strong shelf presence |

**Anti-slop check:** Every color choice needs a rationale tied to brand strategy, not "blue because it looks nice."

## Typography Pairing Principles

### Pairing Strategies
1. **Contrast:** Serif heading + sans-serif body (classic, readable)
2. **Harmony:** Same family, different weights (cohesive, modern)
3. **Personality:** Display/decorative heading + clean body (distinctive, risky)

### Font Personality Map

| Font Style | Personality | Example Brands |
|-----------|------------|----------------|
| Geometric sans (Futura, Montserrat) | Modern, clean, confident | Spotify, Airbnb |
| Humanist sans (Open Sans, Lato) | Friendly, approachable, warm | Google, Amazon |
| Grotesque sans (Helvetica, Inter) | Neutral, professional, Swiss | Apple, Uber |
| Old-style serif (Garamond, Caslon) | Traditional, trustworthy, literary | Tiffany, Harper's |
| Transitional serif (Times, Georgia) | Authoritative, established | NYT, gov sites |
| Slab serif (Rockwell, Roboto Slab) | Strong, grounded, impactful | Honda, Volvo |
| Monospace (JetBrains Mono, Fira Code) | Technical, developer, precise | GitHub, Stripe |
| Display/Script | Unique, expressive, limited use | Coca-Cola (script only) |

### Rules
- Max 2 font families (3 is acceptable if one is monospace for code)
- Heading and body must have enough contrast to create hierarchy
- Test at small sizes: if body copy is unreadable at 14px, pick a different font
- Always include a system font fallback

## Business-Type Adaptations

### SaaS
- UI screenshots/mockups should be part of the stylescape
- Consider dark mode as a direction
- Developer tools: monospace fonts, code-aesthetic are valid choices
- Dashboard/data visualization style matters

### Local Service
- Local photography > stock photos (always)
- Map/geography elements can be distinctive
- Community imagery over corporate imagery

### Content Creator
- The creator's face/presence is a brand element
- Thumbnail style IS brand identity
- Social media grid appearance matters
- Consider video overlay/title card style

### Ecommerce
- Product photography style is the primary brand carrier
- Packaging design direction should be in the stylescape
- Consider unboxing experience visual language
- Shopping UI elements (product cards, cart, checkout) matter
