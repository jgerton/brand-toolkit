# April Dunford's Positioning Framework

Source: "Obviously Awesome" by April Dunford

## Core Principle

Positioning defines how your product is a leader at delivering something that a well-defined set of customers cares a lot about. It is context-setting for products, like the opening scene of a movie that orients the audience.

Good positioning sets off assumptions about your product that are true. Bad positioning sets off assumptions that aren't true, leaving your teams to undo the damage.

---

## The 5 Components (in order)

Components are interdependent. The flow MUST start with competitive alternatives. Starting elsewhere produces positioning that sounds good internally but fails with customers.

### 1. Competitive Alternatives

**Question:** What would customers do if your solution didn't exist?

**Key nuances:**
- This is NOT "list every possible competitor." It's what customers actually consider or currently do.
- "Do nothing" is valid. It means the customer sticks with their current way: spreadsheets, manual processes, hiring an intern.
- Avoid "phantom competitors": companies that theoretically compete but you never actually lose deals to.
- In enterprise software, ~25% of deals are lost to "no decision." Position against the status quo.

**Business-type adaptations:**
- **SaaS:** Other software, manual processes, spreadsheets, hiring someone to do it
- **Local service:** DIY, a different type of provider, ignoring the problem
- **Content creator:** Other creators in the niche, free content, books, courses
- **Ecommerce:** Direct competitors, Amazon, DIY/homemade, going without

### 2. Unique Attributes (Key Differentiators)

**Question:** What do you have that the alternatives do NOT?

**Key nuances:**
- List everything unique: features, business model, supply chain, proprietary process, IP, partnerships
- Don't judge value yet. Even "complexity" can be valuable in the right context.
- Focus on what you have, not what you lack
- Include non-feature differentiators: pricing model, delivery method, community, expertise

### 3. Value (for customers)

**Question:** So what? What can those unique attributes DO for customers?

**Key nuances:**
- Customers don't care about features. They care what features do for them.
- Map each unique attribute to the value it enables
- Value can come from a combination of attributes, not just individual ones
- Keep an open mind about which customers perceive this value

**Anti-slop check:** Every value claim must trace back to a specific unique attribute. If it can't, it's generic.

### 4. Best-Fit Customers (Who Cares A Lot)

**Question:** Who are the customers that care the MOST about your differentiated value?

**Key nuances:**
- Many prospects care somewhat. You want the ones who care A LOT.
- These customers understand your value quickly, purchase faster, and rarely ask for discounts
- Get specific beyond demographics: what situation, trigger, or pain makes them care?
- "Small businesses" is too broad. Which small businesses? What makes the ones who love you different?

**Business-type adaptations:**
- **SaaS:** Role + company size + specific pain point + tech stack
- **Local service:** Geography + property type + trigger event (just moved, renovation, seasonal)
- **Content creator:** Skill level + goal + current frustration
- **Ecommerce:** Lifestyle + values + purchase trigger + price sensitivity

### 5. Market Category

**Question:** What's the context that makes your value obvious to your best-fit customers?

**Key nuances:**
- This is the frame of reference. When you declare a category, customers make assumptions about competitors, features, and pricing.
- You can position in an existing category (leverage what customers already know) or create a new one (expensive, risky, requires educating the market)
- 90% of tech IPOs in the last 5 years positioned in existing markets, not new categories
- A niche of an existing category is usually better than inventing a new one for startups
- Test: does this category make your unique value OBVIOUS, or does it obscure it?

**Three positioning styles:**
1. **Head-to-head:** Take on the leader in an existing category (need clear differentiators)
2. **Niche:** Own a subsegment of an existing category (best for startups)
3. **New category:** Invent a new frame of reference (most expensive, highest risk/reward)

## The Exercise (Workshop Format)

### Setup
- Cross-functional team: marketing, sales, product, founder
- 3-4 hours minimum
- Whiteboard or shared doc

### Step 1: Let Go
- List where the product idea started. Acknowledge it.
- Now set it aside. The original vision may not be the best positioning today.

### Step 2: Competitive Alternatives
- Ask: "If we didn't exist, what would our best customers do?"
- List real alternatives your customers actually evaluate (not phantom competitors)
- Include "do nothing / status quo"

### Step 3: Unique Attributes
- For each alternative: "What do we have that they don't?"
- Be exhaustive. Features, business model, team, process, partnerships.

### Step 4: Value
- For each unique attribute: "So what? What does this enable for customers?"
- Map attributes to outcomes customers care about

### Step 5: Best-Fit Customers
- "Who cares about this value MORE than anyone else?"
- Get specific: industry, role, situation, trigger, company characteristics

### Step 6: Market Category
- "What frame of reference makes our value most obvious?"
- Generate 3-5 candidate categories
- Test each: does it highlight our strengths or obscure them?
- Choose the one where your differentiated value is most obvious

### Step 7: Document and Test
- Write it down with date
- Test with real prospects
- Monitor for market shifts that weaken the position

## Common Traps

1. **Defining alternatives too broadly:** Listing every possible competitor instead of what customers actually consider
2. **Assuming you must create a new category:** Niche positioning in existing categories is safer and cheaper
3. **Confusing positioning with messaging:** Positioning is the strategic foundation. Messaging is built ON TOP of positioning.
4. **One-and-done:** Markets change. Revisit positioning quarterly.
5. **Not involving sales:** Sales knows what alternatives come up in real conversations

## Validation Symptoms

| Symptom | Likely Cause |
|---------|-------------|
| Prospects say "So what exactly is this?" | Weak market category |
| Long sales cycles, lots of "no decision" | Not targeting best-fit customers |
| Constant comparison to wrong competitors | Wrong market category frame |
| Prospects love the demo but don't buy | Value not connected to their specific pain |
| Heavy discounting to close | Not reaching customers who truly value what you offer |
